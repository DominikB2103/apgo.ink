One Goal, Two Stars: Spain Outlast Argentina to Reclaim the World Cup

Ferran Torres’s 106th-minute finish settled a final built on Spanish pressure and Argentine resistance. The 1–0 victory gave Spain a second men’s title—and completed an unusually concentrated run of international success.

APGO Football Desk20 July 20266 min readUpdated 20 July 2026
Reporting basis

Analysis of the primary sources listed on this page. No on-location reporting or original interviews were conducted for this edition.

In this article

Spain are world champions for the second time. The fact is simple; the route to it was not. For 90 minutes at New York New Jersey Stadium on Sunday, 19 July, Spain could not turn control into a goal and Argentina could not turn resistance into a counterstroke. The defending champions held the final at 0–0 through regulation time. Then, in the 106th minute, substitute Ferran Torres scored the only goal of the match. Spain won 1–0 after extra time, sixteen years after Andrés Iniesta's goal delivered the country's first men's World Cup in Johannesburg.

FIFA's official tournament release, timestamped 22:45 on the night of the final, confirms the score, scorer and timing. It also fixes the result in the larger history of this competition: Spain are the winners of the first 48-team, 104-match men's World Cup. Argentina leave as runners-up after coming within one extra-time goal of defending the title they won in Qatar in 2022.

A final of pressure and refusal

The narrow score should not be confused with an even distribution of initiative. Spain's game was organised around having the ball, moving Argentina's defensive block and beginning again when a route closed. Argentina's task became increasingly austere: protect the centre, survive each Spanish phase and wait for a transition that rarely developed. The match reached half-time scoreless and remained that way through the end of regulation.

The Royal Spanish Football Federation's detailed match account records 15 Spanish attempts, ten on target, before extra time, and no Argentine attempt in the first 90 minutes. Emiliano Martínez's goalkeeping kept the final alive. Deep in second-half stoppage time, Enzo Fernández received a second yellow card, leaving Argentina with ten players for extra time. Those details describe the contest more usefully than possession alone: Spain accumulated pressure; Argentina repeatedly absorbed it.

Spain's bench eventually changed the geometry. Pedri and Ferran Torres had entered in the 62nd minute; Nico Williams and Mikel Merino followed in the 75th. According to the federation's match account, Williams supplied the ball that Torres converted shortly after the second period of extra time began. It was Spain's one successful finish, and the only one the final required.

A title built from prevention

Spain's tournament will be remembered for more than the final goal. FIFA's match report says Luis de la Fuente's team set a World Cup record by winning the trophy having conceded only once. That defensive record complicates the familiar shorthand about Spanish possession. The ball was not simply an aesthetic preference; it was also a form of protection. Opponents spent long stretches without the means to build attacks, while Spain's structure behind the ball restricted the damage when possession was lost.

The final extended that logic to its limit. Argentina had scored freely earlier in the competition, but the final offered almost no attacking margin. Spain did not need a cascade of chances to become champions. It needed to prevent Argentina from creating a comparable volume and to stay patient long enough for one late chance to become decisive.

This was Spain's second men's World Cup, after 2010. RFEF places the country level with Uruguay and France on two titles, behind Argentina, Italy, Germany and Brazil. The victory also made Spain the first country to hold the men's and women's World Cup trophies at the same time: Spain's women won their title in 2023.

For De la Fuente, the final was his 50th match as national-team coach. RFEF notes that the World Cup completes a sequence of three major honours under him: the UEFA Nations League in 2023, UEFA Euro 2024 and now the 2026 World Cup. No single match proves the permanence of a footballing era, but three trophies across three different competitions establish more than a brief tournament surge.

The awards followed the result

FIFA's post-final awards reinforced how much of the tournament Spain controlled. Captain Rodri received the Golden Ball as the tournament's best player. Unai Simón won the Golden Glove, and 19-year-old centre-back Pau Cubarsí received the Young Player Award. Kylian Mbappé, whose France side lost 2–0 to Spain in the semi-final, won the Golden Boot. The distribution matters: Spain's title was represented by its central midfielder, goalkeeper and a teenage defender, not only by the forward who scored the winning goal.

Torres nevertheless owns the final's defining action. A substitute decided a match in which Spain's starting attack had spent 90 minutes meeting Martínez and a crowded Argentine defence. It is tempting to turn that into a story about destiny, but the more useful explanation is structural. De la Fuente could introduce senior attackers without abandoning the team's method. Williams widened the field; Torres occupied the finish; the pressure continued until Argentina's resistance finally broke.

The result does not settle every story surrounding the finalists. It should not be used to announce Lionel Messi's international retirement before he does so himself, nor to assume that a young Spanish core guarantees future titles. What it settles is substantial enough. On 19 July 2026, Spain beat Argentina 1–0 after extra time, conceded only one goal across the tournament and added a second star to the one won in South Africa. In a competition enlarged almost everywhere else, the final came down to the smallest possible difference.
