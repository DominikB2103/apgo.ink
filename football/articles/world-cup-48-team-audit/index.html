The 48-Team World Cup Is Over. Its Scale Was the Story.

More countries reached the stage, more people entered the grounds and the scoring rate rose. The first expanded World Cup answered the simplest sporting objection—but left harder questions about format, travel and workload for a calmer audit.

APGO Football Desk20 July 20267 min readUpdated 20 July 2026
Reporting basis

Analysis of the primary sources listed on this page. No on-location reporting or original interviews were conducted for this edition.

In this article

The first 48-team men's World Cup ended on 19 July with Spain lifting the trophy in New Jersey. By the following morning, the outline of the tournament could be expressed in a set of unusually large numbers: 104 matches, 308 goals, 6,810,966 spectators, 16 stadiums, three host countries and 39 days. Those are FIFA's final figures, published hours after Spain's 1–0 extra-time victory over Argentina. They describe a tournament that did not merely add participants. It changed the physical scale of the World Cup.

The expansion from 32 to 48 teams produced 62.5 per cent more matches than Qatar 2022, which had 64. Yet scoring did not decline under the additional weight. The 308 goals in 2026 work out to 2.96 per match. FIFA's 2022 annual report records 172 goals in 64 matches, or 2.69 per match. On the organiser's totals, the scoring rate rose by about ten per cent. That calculation does not measure match quality, but it answers one narrow pre-tournament fear: adding teams did not produce a lower-goal competition overall.

Expansion changed who got through

The new format placed 48 teams in 12 groups of four. The top two in each group and the eight strongest third-place teams advanced, creating a new Round of 32. That meant 72 group matches—eight more matches than the entire 2022 tournament—and it meant two-thirds of the field survived the first phase. The eventual finalists each had to play eight times rather than the traditional seven.

The access argument for expansion is clearest in the group-stage record. FIFA reported that 13 UEFA teams, nine CAF teams, five CONMEBOL teams, three Concacaf teams and two AFC teams reached the knockouts. Nine of Africa's ten entrants advanced; before 2026, no more than two African teams had reached a World Cup knockout phase. Bosnia and Herzegovina, Cabo Verde, Canada, Congo DR, Côte d'Ivoire, Egypt and South Africa all reached the knockouts for the first time.

Four countries—Cabo Verde, Curaçao, Jordan and Uzbekistan—made their World Cup debuts. Cabo Verde progressed unbeaten from its group. Those are not incidental additions to a tournament otherwise unchanged. They are the central sporting case for 48 teams: qualification widened the map, and several newcomers did more than complete the fixture list.

There was evidence of competitive strain, including Germany's 7–1 win over Curaçao, but the group phase was not defined by serial mismatches. Forty-seven of the 48 teams scored. The 72 group matches produced 215 goals, just under three per game. FIFA's Technical Study Group argued during the tournament that the quality gap had narrowed; because that group is part of FIFA, its verdict is not independent, but the basic results support its narrower observation that lower-ranked entrants were often competitive.

More football, not merely more fixtures

FIFA's technical analysts also identified changes in how matches were played. At the end of the group stage, winning teams were regaining the ball on average four seconds faster than losing teams. Before the final, Arsène Wenger, FIFA's chief of global football development, said goals from outside the penalty area had doubled compared with Qatar 2022. Both figures come from the organiser's own performance analysis and will need fuller technical publication before they can carry much explanatory weight. Still, they suggest the extra matches did not simply reproduce the same tactical pattern at greater length.

Crowds supplied the clearest measure of demand. Group-stage attendance reached 4,644,549, with FIFA reporting 99.7 per cent occupancy and an average gate of 64,508. The final total of 6,810,966 across 104 matches gives an average of about 65,490 spectators per match. The cumulative record became much more likely once the tournament added 40 games and used large North American stadiums, but near-capacity occupancy was not guaranteed. It shows that the additional inventory found an audience.

It is important to keep the claim precise. Attendance is not the same as accessibility. A sold seat says nothing by itself about price, travel cost or who was unable to attend. Nor does a record aggregate crowd prove that every host city received a lasting public benefit. FIFA's release is a prompt post-event count, not an audited economic, environmental or social-impact report.

The questions the scoreboard cannot answer

The tournament's geography is the next part of the audit. Matches were spread across Canada, Mexico and the United States rather than concentrated in one compact host. FIFA's immediate summary celebrates three-country reach and 16 stadiums, but does not quantify team flight mileage, supporter travel, emissions or the public cost of security and transport. Those questions require host-city and government reporting that will arrive after the closing ceremony, not a verdict written the next morning.

Workload also remains unresolved. An eight-match route to the title adds one more high-intensity fixture for the finalists, while the 39-day event sits inside a crowded club and international calendar. FIFA has publicly supported a minimum of 72 hours between matches and a 21-day end-of-season rest period, but a genuine assessment must examine what players actually received before and after this tournament. The existence of a rule or policy ambition is not evidence of recovery.

The group format deserves similar scrutiny. Allowing 32 of 48 teams into the knockouts broadened hope deep into the third matchday, but it also eliminated only one-third of the field in the opening phase and required a best-third-place table spanning 12 groups. FIFA selected groups of four partly to reduce collusion risk and preserve three guaranteed games. Whether the resulting balance was clearer or fairer for supporters and teams should be assessed with the complete match data, not only with the number of goals.

The defensible verdict on 20 July is therefore narrower than FIFA's own declaration of a great success, but still significant. The expanded World Cup did not collapse under its size. It delivered a higher scoring rate than 2022, brought seven nations into the knockouts for the first time, filled seats at extraordinary scale and ended with the world's two top-ranked teams contesting a one-goal final. The harder test begins now: separating what was genuinely broadened from what was merely enlarged.
