The ledger they buried

Inside an offshore network moving billions past sanctions — and the six-month paper trail that maps how shell entities quietly route money around export controls.

Mara Okafor · Geneva · 20 June 2026 · 14 min read

Geneva — The ledger arrived as a single encrypted file: 3.4 gigabytes of scanned filings and spreadsheet exports, with no covering note. It took six months to turn it into something defensible — a map of how at least 4.7 billion dollars in restricted trade was routed through companies that, on paper, did almost nothing at all. This is an account of what the documents show, what they do not, and how we checked.

Meridian did not take the file at its word. Every entity named here was traced back to a public registry; every payment chain was rebuilt from transfer metadata before any conclusion was drawn. Where a claim could not be stood up, we say so plainly.

A mailbox that holds twelve hundred companies

The network begins, improbably, at a single street address — a registered-agent office on a quiet commercial road where 1,200 companies share one mailbox and one phone line. None of them keep staff there. A reporter who visited found a reception desk, a wall of pigeonholes, and a manager who declined to discuss individual clients but confirmed the volume.

Shell companies are not, in themselves, illegal. They become a problem when their only function is to put distance between a regulated transaction and the people who profit from it. The filings show entities formed in batches of ten or twelve on the same day, with the same nominee director and bank accounts opened within a fortnight of incorporation.

  • 41 companies traced to a single corporate-services provider.
  • 17 shared at least one nominee director with another entity in the chain.
  • 9 listed a residential apartment as their principal place of business.
  • 0 filed audited accounts in the period reviewed.

How the money actually moved

The mechanism is less a conspiracy than a routine. A buyer subject to export controls cannot pay a manufacturer directly, so the payment is broken into legs. An intermediary in a third country places the order; a second takes delivery and re-invoices; the goods are re-labelled as a permitted category somewhere between the two. Each leg, viewed alone, looks unremarkable. Stacked together, they defeat the control.

You do not need to break a rule if you can break the transaction into pieces small enough that no single piece looks like the rule.Former bank compliance officer, on the record

That former compliance officer, who agreed to be named in our records but asked that the bank not be, walked us through three chains line by line. In each, the final counterparty appeared on a consolidated export-control list under a different corporate identity than the one that placed the first order.

Document

Invoice #GE-2291, dated 14 March 2026, bills "industrial valves, assorted" at 1.2 million dollars. The matching shipping manifest, filed the same week, lists controlled flow-control components destined for a designated end user. The price, weight and container number match exactly.

Following the paper, not the rumour

Investigations of this kind drown in plausible-sounding rumour. We held to a simple rule: a claim entered the published story only when a document or a reproducible data trail supported it. The leaked ledger was the starting point, never the proof.

When a system is designed so that no one person ever sees the whole transaction, "we followed our procedures" can be true and beside the point at the same time.

The pattern in the data is consistent: the longer the chain, the harder it is to see the controlled end. Roughly two-thirds of the flows we rebuilt passed through three or more intermediaries before reaching a counterparty of interest.

What the firms say

Meridian put detailed findings to every named entity ten days before publication. Most did not respond. One corporate-services provider said it complied with all applicable law and conducted standard due diligence. A trade-finance broker disputed the authenticity of an internal memo in our possession; we have set that document aside and it does not support any finding in this story.

Who it touches, and why it matters

Sanctions are blunt instruments, and reasonable people argue about whether they work. But the public bargain behind them is simple: a government tells its citizens that certain goods will not reach certain buyers. When the filings show that bargain quietly failing, the failure is itself the story — regardless of where one stands on the policy.

The 4.7 billion-dollar figure is conservative. It counts only chains we could rebuild end to end from the metadata; partial chains, and transfers we could not match to a controlled end user, were excluded. The true volume is almost certainly larger. We publish the smaller, defensible number.

How we reported this

Records requested

Meridian filed nine records requests across four jurisdictions. Seven were answered; two registries had not responded by publication. Every named entity received detailed findings ten days in advance.

What we verified — and did not

We published a claim only when a document or reproducible data trail supported it. We could not establish who commissioned the original incorporations. One disputed memo was set aside entirely.
