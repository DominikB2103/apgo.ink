The Debt Problem Is Now a Calendar

The world’s borrowing debate usually begins with how much is owed. In 2026, the sharper risk is when that debt matures, what rate replaces it and how much public policy gets crowded out during the rollover.

APGO Economics Desk20 July 20266 min readUpdated 20 July 2026
In this article

There is no single global debt crisis in July 2026. Bond markets are functioning, many borrowers retain access to finance and measured volatility has often remained low. That relative calm is precisely why the more important change can be missed. Debt accumulated during the cheap-money years is being refinanced into a more expensive world. The risk sits not only on a balance sheet but on a calendar.

The scale of that calendar is immense. The OECD’s Global Debt Report 2026 puts the combined sovereign and corporate bond market at $109 trillion. Governments and companies are projected to borrow $29 trillion from bond markets in 2026, $4 trillion more than in 2024 and roughly twice the amount raised a decade earlier. Most government issuance is not new spending: 78 percent of borrowing by OECD governments this year is expected to refinance existing debt.

How higher rates enter the budget

Refinancing sounds administrative. Economically, it is the mechanism through which higher rates reach the budget. A ten-year bond issued at a very low coupon does not become expensive the day a central bank raises its policy rate. It becomes expensive when it matures and must be replaced, or when new deficits are funded at current yields. The International Monetary Fund estimates that global interest payments rose from about 2 percent to nearly 3 percent of GDP in only four years as maturing obligations were rolled over at higher rates.

That shift changes what a debt ratio means. The IMF says global public debt rose to just under 94 percent of GDP in 2025 and is projected to reach 100 percent by 2029. But two governments with the same debt ratio can face very different risks. One may borrow in its own currency at long maturities from a stable domestic investor base. Another may depend on short-term foreign-currency debt and fickle external demand. The stock is a headline; maturity, currency, coupon and ownership determine the transmission.

Borrowers have responded rationally to expensive long-term money by issuing at shorter maturities. That lowers the immediate coupon. It also forces them to return to the market sooner. The OECD warns that 30-year yields rose across most member countries in 2025 even as shorter rates stabilized, encouraging this shift. Saving money now can concentrate rollover risk in the next few budgets.

The buyer side is changing too. Central banks, once unusually large domestic holders of government bonds after years of asset purchases, have been reducing holdings. New supply must increasingly be absorbed by investors that are more sensitive to price, leverage and short-term returns. That does not guarantee a market break. It means governments may need to pay more—or adjust policy faster—when those investors demand a larger risk premium.

Crowding out, not collapse

For richer sovereigns, the first symptom is often crowding out rather than default. Interest takes a larger share of revenue that could have financed infrastructure, social protection, defense or climate adaptation. Political systems then face choices they often prefer to postpone: raise taxes, slow spending, accept higher debt or try to grow faster. None is painless, and growth-enhancing public investment can be the easiest item to cut even when it would improve the long-run denominator of the debt ratio.

The pressure is harsher in developing economies. The World Bank’s International Debt Report 2025 records $8.9 trillion in external debt for low- and middle-income countries at the end of 2024. Those countries paid a record $415 billion in interest. The 78 countries eligible for the World Bank’s cheapest financing and grants owed $1.2 trillion. These are aggregate figures, not proof that every country is insolvent, but they show how much income is already leaving before governments fund domestic priorities.

Weak growth makes the arithmetic worse. The World Bank’s June 2026 Global Economic Prospects projected world growth of 2.5 percent for the year, the weakest pace outside a global recession in nearly two decades. It estimated that by the end of 2026 one quarter of developing economies, one third of low-income economies and half of fragile and conflict-affected economies would still be poorer per person than in 2019. A country cannot easily grow out of a rising interest bill when its income base has not recovered.

Transparency is part of the price. A World Bank analysis of episodes in which hidden public liabilities were revealed found significant increases in sovereign spreads. The logic is straightforward: undisclosed debt deprives citizens of oversight and lenders of a reliable picture of repayment capacity. When the missing obligations surface, markets reprice not only the amount but the credibility of the reporting system.

Managing the schedule

The practical response is less theatrical than declaring debt either harmless or catastrophic. Debt managers can smooth maturity profiles, build local-currency markets and avoid concentrating redemptions in a single year. Governments can publish complete liabilities, including guarantees and state-owned-enterprise exposures. Fiscal adjustment can protect high-return investment rather than cutting every line equally. Where debt is already unsustainable, earlier restructuring is usually less destructive than repeated short extensions that preserve the fiction of solvency.

Borrowing remains essential. States need it to absorb shocks and finance assets whose benefits last for decades. The danger is pretending that the terms do not matter. In 2026, the quiet fiscal event is an old bond coming due. Each rollover imports today’s price of money into tomorrow’s public choices. The debt problem is therefore not one number. It is a schedule of decisions, and the schedule is getting crowded.
