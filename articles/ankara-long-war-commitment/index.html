Ankara’s Long-War Commitment

NATO has turned support for Ukraine into a two-year industrial pledge while expanding its eastern defences. The strategy is becoming more durable just as UN monitors record the worst monthly civilian toll since spring 2022.

APGO World Desk20 July 20267 min readUpdated 20 July 2026
Reporting basis

Analysis of the primary sources listed on this page. No on-location reporting or original interviews were conducted for this edition.

In this article

The most consequential line in NATO’s Ankara Summit Declaration was a number followed by a date. On 8 July, allied leaders pledged €70 billion in military equipment, assistance and training for Ukraine in 2026 and affirmed national commitments to sustain at least an equivalent level in 2027. The declaration also announced more than $50 billion in new alliance procurement and said European allies and Canada had increased spending on core defence requirements by more than $139 billion in 2025.

This is not simply another emergency package. It links Ukraine’s defence to the expansion of allied manufacturing, common procurement and a broader modernization programme spanning air and missile defence, long-range strike, uncrewed systems, space, cyber and intelligence. The European Union’s parallel €90 billion Ukraine Support Loan for 2026–27 gives that architecture a fiscal base: €60 billion is allocated to defence support and €30 billion to general budget assistance. On 30 June, the European Commission released €3.9 billion for drone procurement, part of €28.3 billion intended to support Ukraine’s defence-industrial capacity in 2026.

The money arrived against a worsening civilian picture. The UN Human Rights Monitoring Mission in Ukraine verified at least 293 civilians killed and 1,990 injured in June. The combined figure was 10 per cent higher than in May, 37 per cent higher than in June 2025, and the largest monthly total since April 2022. At a 9 July Security Council briefing, UN officials described a recent Russian attack involving more than 70 missiles and nearly 500 drones; at least 31 people were reportedly killed and more than 200 injured. Those figures were presented as reported preliminary consequences, while the monthly UN monitoring totals are individually verified.

From supplying Ukraine to reorganizing the alliance

NATO’s military posture now extends well beyond weapons transfers. As of June, the alliance maintained nine multinational forward land battlegroups from Bulgaria and Romania to the Baltic and Arctic approaches. The newest, in Finland, is led by Sweden. NATO says its Eastern Sentry activity, launched after a series of reported airspace violations in September 2025, brings additional aircraft, air defence, surveillance and naval assets into a more integrated eastern posture.

The contest is also moving across domains. On 13 July, the North Atlantic Council accused Russia of persistent malicious cyber activity against allied and partner infrastructure and government entities. It said NATO had strengthened the integration of cyber effects into operations and was prepared to use a full range of capabilities in response, in accordance with international law. These are alliance allegations and policy declarations; the statement did not publish technical evidence for individual incidents. Their significance lies in NATO placing cyber campaigns within its deterrence framework rather than treating them solely as criminal or intelligence matters.

Moscow reads the same developments as escalation. In its 9 July statement to the Security Council, Russia’s UN mission cited the Ankara pledge as evidence of direct Western participation in the war and said Russia had no basis to stop pursuing its stated military goals. It denied targeting civilian objects and framed its attacks as strikes on military-industrial, airfield, fuel and logistics infrastructure. Those are Russian government claims. They conflict with Ukrainian accounts, with allied governments’ statements and with the UN’s documented pattern of civilian harm. Including them is necessary to identify the escalation logic, not to treat competing claims as equally verified.

The diplomatic distance is therefore measurable. NATO defines long-term funding as the means to protect Ukrainian sovereignty and strengthen deterrence. Russia defines that funding as proof that the alliance is a party to a wider confrontation. Meanwhile, European Union sanctions adopted on 17 July targeted one person and five entities tied to Russian drone manufacturing after deadly strikes on Kyiv earlier in the month. Each side treats the other’s next increment of military capacity as confirmation of its prior threat assessment.

Analysis: durable support is a strategy, but not yet a settlement

Ankara marks a shift from annual uncertainty towards a rolling industrial commitment. That can improve planning: Ukraine can contract for systems, suppliers can expand production, and European governments can coordinate air defence, ammunition and drone orders over more than one budget cycle. It may also reduce Moscow’s incentive to wait for a single election or appropriations fight to interrupt support.

But durability creates its own risks. The declaration offers a funding horizon, not a political end-state. It does not set an accession timetable for Ukraine, define conditions for a ceasefire, or explain how deterrence, territorial questions and security guarantees would fit together in negotiations. Those omissions are not evidence of secret policy; they show what the published document does not resolve.

There is also a growing boundary problem. Air defence and forward battlegroups are described as defensive, yet deep-strike capabilities, cyber effects and attacks far behind the front can be perceived by an adversary as part of one connected escalation ladder. Perception does not determine legality, nor does it erase responsibility for aggression. It does affect crisis stability. Clear command arrangements, attribution standards and military communication channels matter more as the alliance’s support system becomes faster and more integrated.

The civilian data impose a harder test than spending totals. If the new commitments are to serve their stated protective purpose, delivery must be judged partly by whether Ukraine receives enough interceptors, warning systems and resilient energy equipment to reduce harm away from the front. Offensive production can change battlefield calculations; civilian protection determines whether a long-war strategy preserves the society it is intended to defend.

What should be watched next is concrete. Do allies convert the €70 billion pledge into delivered systems rather than announced contracts? Does the EU loan accelerate production without weakening oversight? Do June’s record casualty levels persist through the summer? And can any diplomatic channel establish rules for attacks near nuclear sites, critical infrastructure and NATO borders before another airspace or cyber incident forces leaders to make decisions under pressure?

Ankara’s answer to uncertainty is endurance. It is a credible organizing principle for deterrence. It is not, on its own, a theory of peace.
