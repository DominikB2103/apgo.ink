The Sun Also Rises in its 100th year: expatriates, war and Pamplona

Published on 22 October 1926, Hemingway’s first novel will reach its centenary this autumn. Its routes through Paris and Pamplona still shape the expatriate myth.

APGO20 July 20266 min read

Source noteThe public sources and documents listed below form the basis of this article. APGO conducted no original interviews or on-location reporting.

Charles Scribner's Sons published Ernest Hemingway's The Sun Also Rises on 22 October 1926. The John F. Kennedy Presidential Library dates its composition from July 1925 to April 1926. Hemingway was drawing on a recent journey: he and Hadley Richardson Hemingway had first visited Pamplona in 1923, and a larger trip to the San Fermín festival in 1925 supplied people, quarrels and places that he reshaped for the novel.

The book follows Jake Barnes, an American journalist living in Paris, and a circle of expatriates who travel through France and Spain. Their route gives the story momentum. It also keeps returning them to the same injuries and rivalries. As the centenary approaches, the streets, hotels, trains and cafés remain easy to picture. The conduct of the people moving through them is harder to turn into a postcard.

Travel provides the machinery

Robert Cohn introduces travel as a proposed cure. After reading W. H. Hudson's The Purple Land, he wants Jake to leave Paris for South America. Jake dismisses the hope that a new country will produce a new life. The exchange lays down a pattern that the rest of the book tests. Characters change cities, rooms and companions while carrying their grievances with them.

The itinerary runs from Paris night life to the quieter fishing country near Burguete, then into Pamplona for the festival. Jake notices prices, meals, luggage, rail connections and who pays. His work as a journalist is part of his way of seeing. The practical details give the movement weight and let social tensions emerge through small transactions. Cohn follows Brett Ashley to San Sebastián. Mike Campbell needles Cohn in Pamplona. Jake, who knows the terrain and the rituals, still helps arrange choices that hurt people close to him.

Travel also sorts the group into temporary alliances. Jake and Bill Gorton find an easier companionship while fishing. The festival brings everyone back into crowded rooms, streets and cafés, where privacy becomes scarce and insults turn public. After the fiesta, the party disperses. Jake goes to San Sebastián and is soon summoned to Madrid by Brett. Distance has postponed the central relationship without changing its terms.

The war stays in the room

Hemingway placed Gertrude Stein's phrase “You are all a lost generation” before the first chapter. He paired it with lines from Ecclesiastes about generations passing and the earth remaining. The novel then gives the broad label a precise physical source. Jake tells a prospective partner, “I got hurt in the war.” His injury has altered his sexual life and made a conventional future with Brett impossible.

The book rarely explains that injury at length. It records its effects in rooms, glances and clipped conversations. Jake and Brett love one another, sleep separately and repeatedly return to the subject through indirection. Brett seeks other men, and Jake sometimes facilitates the arrangements that torment him. Emotion accumulates in the gaps between the short sentences and in what the characters cannot settle in speech.

The group's talk carries other forms of damage. Cohn is repeatedly marked as Jewish and subjected to antisemitic description and abuse. Brett's freedom attracts the men around her, then becomes material for accusation and control. Fights and drinking offer a public language for conflicts that the characters handle poorly in private. Those elements belong to the novel's social world and remain present even when editions, adaptations or tourist accounts foreground the scenery.

Pamplona and the reading public

The Festival of San Fermín had a long local history before Hemingway arrived. His novel changed the scale of its international audience. Visit Navarra, the regional tourist board, credits The Sun Also Rises with the festival's definitive global popularisation. Pamplona now includes Hemingway in the way it presents the city to visitors, and readers continue to arrive looking for places named in the book.

The festival chapters help explain that influence. Hemingway gives the celebration a daily rhythm of rockets, processions, music, meals, bull runs and afternoons at the ring. Crowds compress the characters' private disputes into a public spectacle. Jake's knowledge of bullfighting grants him standing with aficionados and introduces Brett to the young matador Pedro Romero. Admiration soon becomes another source of betrayal within the group.

The novel also records the cleanup. Once the festival ends, the square empties. Children collect rocket sticks, and a waiter removes notices from a café window. These modest details return Pamplona to ordinary time after days of noise. They keep the setting from becoming a continuous performance staged for Jake and his companions.

The last journey

The final trip is to Madrid. Brett has left Romero and asks Jake for help. He finds her alone at the Hotel Montana, and they drink and eat before taking a taxi through the city. Their conversation circles once more around the life they might have shared. Brett imagines that it could have been good. Jake answers, “Isn't it pretty to think so?”

That line closes the novel on a conditional future that never becomes a plan. The taxi continues through Madrid with a mounted policeman beside it and Brett sitting close to Jake. After journeys across France and Spain, the pair occupy another moving vehicle and have no new destination beyond the immediate ride. As the centenary approaches, the printed book still returns readers to a conversation that ends before the taxi stops.
