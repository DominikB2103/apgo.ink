The Sun Also Rises Turned Exile Into an Export

A century after publication, Hemingway’s first major novel still shapes how outsiders imagine Paris, Pamplona and expatriate life—even as the book itself is more wounded, exclusionary and skeptical than the lifestyle myth built around it.

APGO Books Desk20 July 20266 min readUpdated 20 July 2026
Reporting basis

Analysis of the primary sources listed on this page. No on-location reporting or original interviews were conducted for this edition.

In this article

On 22 October 2026, The Sun Also Rises will be 100 years old. The centenary belongs not only to a novel but to an export industry of images: zinc café counters, night taxis, trout streams, white trousers, red wine, bullrings and the promise that a life can become more authentic by being moved abroad. Long before travel feeds and location tags, Ernest Hemingway produced an itinerary that readers could mistake for a way of life.

The archive supplies a useful corrective to the legend of effortless prose. The John F. Kennedy Presidential Library dates the writing of the book from July 1925 to April 1926 and its first publication by Charles Scribner’s Sons to 22 October 1926. The library’s Hemingway collection preserves manuscript material and annotated galleys. Whatever velocity the finished sentences project, the book was constructed through drafts, cuts and editorial decisions. Apparent spontaneity was a technical effect.

The itinerary as structure

Its opening pages introduce a group of American and British expatriates circulating through Paris. Jake Barnes works as a journalist. Lady Brett Ashley moves through the same bars and social circles, loved and judged by the men around her. Robert Cohn, an American writer and former boxer, is made both participant and target. The party travels south: first toward fishing country, then Pamplona and the Festival of San Fermín. Movement supplies the novel’s structure, but it does not deliver escape. Jealousy, war damage and social cruelty travel with the characters.

Hemingway frames that tension before the first chapter. One epigraph supplies Gertrude Stein’s phrase “lost generation.” The other turns to Ecclesiastes, where generations pass but the earth abides and the sun rises again. The pairing is more complicated than the label that survived it. One voice declares a historical break; the other places human damage inside a cycle larger than any cohort. The novel’s title comes from the second vision, not the first.

That distinction is easy to lose because the book is so effective at producing surfaces. It names routes, hotels, cafés, drinks and rituals. It shifts from cramped interiors to open country and then to the compressed public theater of the fiesta. Jake’s reporting eye registers prices, meals, train compartments and who pays. The details do not merely describe place. They teach the reader how to move through it.

When literature becomes infrastructure

Pamplona still lives with that success. The official Visit Navarra history of San Fermín credits the 1926 novel with the festival’s definitive internationalization. The city now offers Hemingway routes alongside its own much older religious, civic and regional traditions. Literature did not invent the festival; the running of the bulls and other customs long predated Hemingway’s visits. The novel changed the scale and identity of the outside audience. A local celebration became a global literary destination.

The transformation raises a question that now feels familiar: when does representation become infrastructure? A compelling story changes travel demand, commercial geography and what later visitors expect to see. The bars and streets become evidence of the book; the book becomes a filter through which the place is consumed. A century later, the feedback loop between narrative and destination is standard tourism practice. The Sun Also Rises achieved it without a marketing department.

What the lifestyle myth leaves out

Yet reading the novel as a lifestyle manual requires ignoring much of what it shows. Jake’s wound from the First World War is central but rarely explained directly. The group drinks and travels, but its freedom is repeatedly exposed as evasion. The final exchange between Jake and Brett does not deliver romantic completion; it recognizes an imagined alternative that cannot be lived.

The social world is also ugly in ways a centenary should not polish away. Robert Cohn is subjected to persistent antisemitic description and abuse. Brett’s sexual autonomy is magnetic to the men around her and then used as grounds to punish her. Masculinity is tested through money, fighting, endurance and proximity to the bullring. The narrator’s restraint does not make those forces neutral. It can make them more revealing, because prejudice and domination appear as ordinary habits of the group.

This is why the archival record matters. Manuscripts and galleys remind readers that the omissions are crafted, not empty. Hemingway’s method asks the reader to infer pressure from what characters refuse or fail to say. That method helped change twentieth-century prose, but it also created a durable authorial persona: the reporter-adventurer who could turn lived experience into stripped-down authority. The persona became another export attached to the novel.

The best centenary reading separates that brand from the book without pretending they have nothing to do with each other. The Sun Also Rises manufactured an extraordinarily portable image of expatriate life. It also records people discovering that movement, spectacle and style cannot repair every injury. Its landscapes remain seductive because the novel understands seduction. Its moral atmosphere remains harsh because it understands the cost of turning other people—and other places—into material.

A hundred years on, the book’s contemporary subject is not escape. It is mediation: how a compact narrative can convert a small social circle into a generation, a city festival into a world destination and a writer into a type. The sun rises again; the itinerary is repeated. The more serious question is what each new traveler has been taught not to see.
