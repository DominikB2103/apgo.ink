How printers and riders carried the Declaration across the colonies

The Declaration reached the public through broadsides, newspapers and public readings. Its distribution in 1776 shows how a political text acquired force through circulation.

By APGOPublished 20 July 20266 min read
Article tools

On 5 July 1776, John Hancock began sending printed copies of the Declaration of Independence from Philadelphia. The Continental Congress had approved Richard Henry Lee's resolution for independence on 2 July and adopted the Declaration two days later. John Dunlap, Congress's printer, set the text in type during the night of 4 July. By the following day, his broadsides were on their way to colonial assemblies, conventions, committees of safety and Continental commanders.

That sequence places the familiar parchment in its proper chronology. The large signed sheet now displayed at the National Archives came later, after Hancock's dispatches left Philadelphia. Congress first announced its decision through printed pages built for travel, public display and repeated reading. The surviving Dunlap broadsides record the Declaration at the moment it entered circulation.

The first printed Declaration

Dunlap's edition filled a single large sheet. It carried the heading used by Congress and ended with Hancock's name in type, followed by an attestation from Charles Thomson, the congressional secretary. The rows of signatures known from the parchment were absent. Congress had authorised a text, and the printer's job was to reproduce it accurately enough for officials in distant places to act on it.

The size of Dunlap's press run remains uncertain. The dispatch record gives a clearer account of its purpose. Hancock enclosed copies with letters to political bodies across the colonies. He also sent one to General George Washington in New York. Each recipient could post the sheet, preserve it as an official record, arrange a public reading or provide the words to another printer. One night's work in a Philadelphia print shop supplied the source for many later encounters with the Declaration.

Newspaper columns and public voices

The text reached newspaper readers quickly. On 6 July, the Pennsylvania Evening Post became the first newspaper to publish the Declaration. Its compositors reset Dunlap's lines into the narrower measure of a newspaper page. Other papers followed, giving readers far from Congress access to the complete statement and creating additional copies that could be passed from hand to hand.

Public readings carried the words beyond the audience for print. The Declaration was read in Philadelphia on 8 July. Washington received Hancock's dispatch in New York and ordered the text read to the Continental Army on 9 July. Officers and soldiers heard Congress announce that the colonies regarded themselves as free and independent states while British forces gathered nearby. The army encountered the document through a public voice addressing assembled ranks.

Every stage required a person to make a choice. A rider carried the sheet. An editor gave it space. A compositor reset the type. A local official selected a time and place for a reading, and a reader supplied the voice. Variations and errors could enter during that process, which makes the surviving early printings useful to historians. They show how a congressional act passed through the ordinary labour of eighteenth-century communication.

The parchment followed publication

Congress turned to the ceremonial copy after the first wave of dispatches. On 19 July, it ordered the Declaration engrossed on parchment under a new title. Signing began on 2 August, and other names were added later. The final company of 56 signers was therefore assembled over time. The parchment joined text, title and signatures in the durable arrangement reproduced in textbooks and displayed at the National Archives.

The dates distinguish several decisions that are often compressed into one scene. Congress voted for independence on 2 July, adopted the Declaration on 4 July, sent printed copies on 5 July and ordered the parchment on 19 July. The familiar scene of every delegate crowding around a completed parchment on Independence Day compresses that work into a single moment. The institutional record took shape through votes, printing orders, dispatches and signatures recorded on different days.

Goddard printed the signers

Six months later, Congress commissioned another important edition. Meeting in Baltimore on 18 January 1777, it ordered authenticated copies of the Declaration sent to each state. Mary Katharine Goddard, a Baltimore printer and postmaster, produced the broadside. It was the first printed edition to present the names of the signers. The Library of Congress copy lists them in two columns, with the names grouped by state. Thomas McKean, whose signature was added later, is absent from this edition.

Goddard placed her own name at the foot of the page. Her imprint identifies the shop responsible for an official edition that connected the adopted words with the delegates who had signed them. State governments receiving the broadside could preserve a formally authenticated record drawn from the congressional text, avoiding dependence on a newspaper transcription or a worn copy from the first press run.

The Declaration's first year can be followed through these distinct objects. Dunlap's sheet carried Congress's decision out of Philadelphia. Newspaper pages placed it before a wider reading public. Public officials and military commanders supplied voices and audiences. The engrossed parchment gathered the signatures, and Goddard's broadside sent those names back into print. The dates and surviving pages show a document moving through presses, post roads, meeting places and government offices before the parchment became its dominant public image.
