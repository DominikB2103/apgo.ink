The AI Boom Has Reached the Substation

Data-center demand is growing faster than the power system built to serve it. The decisive questions are no longer confined to chips and models: they are about transformers, transmission, credible forecasts and who pays when a promised load fails to arrive.

APGO Technology Desk20 July 20267 min readUpdated 20 July 2026
In this article

Artificial intelligence is often described in units of models, chips and investment. The power system encounters it in megawatts. A data-center campus may appear in a utility forecast as a single block of demand larger than a town, expected to run around the clock and to connect faster than new transmission or generation can usually be built. The AI contest has reached the substation.

The International Energy Agency’s 2026 update estimates that data centers used about 485 terawatt-hours of electricity worldwide in 2025. Its central projection nearly doubles that figure to 950 TWh in 2030, about 3 percent of global electricity consumption. Demand from AI-focused data centers is projected to triple over the same period. These are scenarios, not meter readings from the future, and the IEA stresses substantial uncertainty. The direction and speed of change are nonetheless already visible: data-center electricity use grew 17 percent in 2025 while total global electricity demand grew 3 percent.

The United States illustrates the range of possible outcomes. A Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory study for the Department of Energy estimated that US data centers consumed 176 TWh in 2023, 4.4 percent of national electricity use, up from 1.9 percent in 2018. Depending on equipment shipments, operating practices and cooling, the report projected 325 to 580 TWh in 2028—between 6.7 and 12 percent of US electricity demand.

Forecasting a load that may move

The width of that range is not a flaw to be edited out. It is the planning problem. Utilities must make long-lived investments before they know which announced campuses will be built, how quickly computing efficiency will improve, how intensively expensive processors will run or whether a customer will relocate. Underbuilding can delay connection and economic activity. Overbuilding can leave ordinary ratepayers paying for wires and generation sized for a load that never materialized.

Efficiency does not automatically settle the question. The electricity required for an individual AI task is falling rapidly, according to the IEA. But cheaper and more capable systems stimulate more uses, including agents that perform repeated or continuous work. Total demand can rise while energy per task falls. This is the familiar rebound problem in a new industrial form: efficiency changes the cost of consumption, not only its footprint.

The grid becomes the compute supply chain

Supplying the power will require a mixed system. The IEA’s 2025 base case projected electricity generation serving data centers to grow from about 460 TWh in 2024 to more than 1,000 TWh in 2030. Renewables provide nearly half the additional supply in that case, followed by natural gas and coal, with nuclear playing a larger role later in the decade. In a faster “lift-off” scenario, long grid-connection queues cause more of the incremental demand to be met by fossil generation. The climate effect therefore depends not just on how much computing is built but on when and where it connects.

Location turns a global percentage into a local constraint. Data centers cluster where fiber, land, tax treatment and existing grid capacity align. A national system might have enough annual energy while a particular region lacks a substation, transformer or transmission path at the requested place and hour. The IEA reported in April 2026 that transformer and gas-turbine supply chains had tightened, while project pipelines were straining planning, connection and permitting systems. Physical equipment and administrative throughput have become part of the compute supply chain.

The US Department of Energy’s draft July 2026 National Transmission Needs Study places new large loads alongside generator interconnection and congestion as reasons for additional transmission. A line on a planning map does not become steel quickly. Routes require studies, cost allocation, permits, equipment, construction and coordination across jurisdictions. A data hall can be assembled faster than the network that makes its power reliable.

Who pays for the connection

That timing mismatch is pushing electricity tariffs into technology policy. Berkeley Lab’s review of large-load rate design documents a range of minimum-demand charges, collateral requirements, long contracts and study-cost provisions. The aim is not to punish a new industry. It is to align risk with the party creating it. If a utility builds for a forecast 500-megawatt campus and the customer ultimately uses half that capacity—or leaves—the unrecovered cost should not silently migrate onto household bills.

Tariffs can also purchase flexibility. A data center that can credibly reduce demand during a tight hour, shift some computing in time, use storage or ramp toward full load gradually is easier to integrate than an inflexible block. Not every task can move; latency-sensitive services and reliability commitments impose limits. Onsite generation can help but may worsen local pollution or evade shared grid costs if rules are poorly designed. Flexibility must be measured, dispatchable and reflected in the contract, not merely promised in a sustainability report.

The most useful policy question is therefore not whether AI uses “too much” electricity in the abstract. It is whether each project brings a believable load forecast, pays the incremental cost it causes, can modify demand during stressed periods and is matched with deliverable supply. Regulators need transparent queues that remove speculative projects. Utilities need better regional forecasts and contracts that protect existing customers. Developers need incentives to locate where capacity, clean generation and water constraints can support them.

AI may also improve the grid—forecasting faults, optimizing operations and extracting more capacity from existing lines. Those gains should be pursued. They do not erase the lead time of transformers or the politics of a transmission route. Intelligence remains embodied in infrastructure.

The AI boom began as a race for compute. Its next phase is a test of public utility governance. The winners will not simply possess the most chips. They will be the places that can turn uncertain, concentrated demand into investments that remain useful even if the technology cycle changes. A substation is less glamorous than a model launch. It is also where the promise becomes load.
