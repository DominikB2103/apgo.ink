Summary

Begin with the reason the document exists. Avoid ornamental language. Give the reader the smallest accurate map of the problem, the result, and the evidence.

For a theorem, state the hypotheses before the conclusion. For a discovery, separate observation from interpretation. For a news entry, explain what changed and why it matters.

Main claim

Theorem or principal statement

Statement. Under clearly specified assumptions, a precise conclusion follows. Replace this with your formal statement or central finding.

Conditions. List the space, model, sample, instrument, or dataset conditions that make the claim valid.

Method

Describe the proof strategy, experimental design, computational procedure, or editorial basis. Keep the method reproducible enough that a reader can identify what would falsify or weaken the claim.

  1. Define the objects or measurements.
  2. State the dependency line: lemmas, assumptions, instruments, or data sources.
  3. Explain the central step without hiding uncertainty.

Evidence and interpretation

The evidence section should distinguish what is directly shown from what is inferred. In a proof note, this may be a lemma chain. In a field report, it may be a record of measurements.

Editorial note

Use notes sparingly. They should clarify uncertainty, scope, or terminology; they should not interrupt the main argument.

References

Reference format

Author. Title. Journal or archive. Year. Link or local citation. Add only references that directly support the document.
