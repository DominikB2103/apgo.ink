Spectral rigidity in compact finite-dimensional models.

A real symmetric operator cannot hide its principal directions: it admits an orthonormal eigenbasis, and its action becomes diagonal after an orthogonal change of coordinates.

Statement

Spectral theorem, finite-dimensional form

Let A be a real symmetric matrix. There exists an orthogonal matrix Q and a real diagonal matrix Λ such that A = QΛQT.

The hypothesis is not decorative. Symmetry gives a stable relationship between the operator and the inner product, forcing eigenvectors associated with distinct eigenvalues to be orthogonal.

Proof strategy

One begins by locating a real eigenvalue through the characteristic polynomial and the variational behaviour of the quadratic form. After choosing a unit eigenvector, its orthogonal complement is invariant under the same operator.

Induction on dimension completes the construction: diagonalise the restriction to the orthogonal complement, then assemble an orthonormal basis for the entire space.

Consequence

The theorem converts a coupled transformation into independent scalar actions along orthogonal directions. This is why symmetric matrices are especially tractable in optimisation, mechanics, statistics, and numerical analysis.

Reading note

When applying the result, check symmetry first. Similar-looking matrices can have complex eigenstructure or fail to admit an orthonormal eigenbasis.

Scope

This note states the finite-dimensional real version only. Infinite-dimensional spectral theory requires additional analytic hypotheses and a more careful distinction between point spectrum and continuous spectrum.
