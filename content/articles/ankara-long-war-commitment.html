NATO leaders set out a two-year commitment to Ukraine at their Ankara summit on 8 July. The declaration pledged €70 billion in military equipment, assistance and training in 2026, then affirmed national commitments to maintain at least the same level in 2027. It also announced more than $50 billion in new alliance procurement and said European allies and Canada had increased spending on core defence requirements by more than $139 billion in 2025.

The declaration connects support for Ukraine with allied manufacturing, common procurement and a modernization programme covering air and missile defence, long-range strike, uncrewed systems, space, cyber and intelligence. The European Union’s €90 billion Ukraine Support Loan for 2026 and 2027 provides €60 billion for defence and €30 billion for general budget assistance. On 30 June, the European Commission released €3.9 billion for drone procurement, part of €28.3 billion intended to support Ukraine’s defence industry in 2026.

The announcement came during a rise in civilian casualties. The UN Human Rights Monitoring Mission in Ukraine verified at least 293 civilians killed and 1,990 injured in June. The combined figure was 10 per cent higher than in May, 37 per cent higher than in June 2025 and the largest monthly total since April 2022. At a 9 July Security Council briefing, UN officials described a recent Russian attack involving more than 70 missiles and nearly 500 drones. At least 31 people were reportedly killed and more than 200 injured. The figures from that attack were preliminary reports. The monthly monitoring totals were individually verified by the UN mission.

From supplying Ukraine to reorganizing the alliance

NATO’s military posture now includes weapons transfers and forces deployed along its eastern flank. As of June, the alliance maintained nine multinational forward land battlegroups from Bulgaria and Romania to the Baltic and Arctic approaches. Sweden leads the newest group in Finland. NATO says Eastern Sentry, launched after reported airspace violations in September 2025, combines additional aircraft, air defence, surveillance and naval assets.

On 13 July, the North Atlantic Council accused Russia of persistent malicious cyber activity against allied and partner infrastructure and government entities. It said NATO had integrated cyber effects more closely into operations and was prepared to use a full range of capabilities in accordance with international law. The statement provided no technical evidence for individual incidents. It placed alleged cyber campaigns within NATO’s deterrence policy instead of addressing them solely as criminal or intelligence matters.

Russia’s UN mission presented the Ankara pledge as evidence of direct Western participation in the war. In a 9 July statement to the Security Council, it said Russia had no basis to stop pursuing its stated military goals. The mission denied targeting civilian objects and described Russian attacks as strikes on military-industrial, airfield, fuel and logistics infrastructure. Those claims conflict with Ukrainian and allied statements and with the civilian harm documented by the UN.

The public positions leave little common ground. NATO says long-term funding will protect Ukrainian sovereignty and strengthen deterrence. Russia describes the same funding as evidence that the alliance has joined a wider confrontation. European Union sanctions adopted on 17 July targeted one person and five entities linked to Russian drone manufacturing after deadly strikes on Kyiv earlier in the month.

Analysis: what the commitment resolves

The two-year commitment gives Ukraine and its suppliers more time to plan. Ukraine can contract for systems, manufacturers can prepare production and European governments can coordinate orders for air defence, ammunition and drones across more than one budget cycle. A rolling commitment may also make it harder for Moscow to base its strategy on a single election or appropriations fight interrupting support.

The published documents leave major political questions open. They give no timetable for Ukrainian accession to NATO or conditions for a ceasefire. They leave the treatment of territorial disputes and security guarantees in negotiations unspecified. Those are limits of the declaration itself.

As the support system expands, military activities in different domains can become harder to separate during a crisis. NATO describes air defence and forward battlegroups as defensive. Deep-strike capabilities, cyber operations and attacks far behind the front may still be perceived by an adversary as connected steps. Legal responsibility remains unchanged by that perception. It can still influence decisions made after an airspace violation or cyber incident. Clear command arrangements, attribution standards and military communication channels become more important as allied operations grow faster and more integrated.

Civilian casualty data provide one measure of the policy’s performance. The stated protective purpose requires enough interceptors, warning systems and resilient energy equipment to reduce harm away from the front. Offensive production may change battlefield calculations. Civilian protection will show whether the strategy is preserving the society that allied governments say they support.

The next evidence will come from deliveries, oversight and casualty figures. Announced contracts will need to become fielded systems. EU loan spending will need to increase production while retaining scrutiny. Summer monitoring will show whether June’s casualty level persists. Military communication channels will face their next test when an airspace, nuclear-site or cyber incident requires leaders to respond under pressure.

Ankara has established a longer funding horizon. Its effect will depend on the systems delivered, the protection they provide and the ability of military channels to contain the next incident.
