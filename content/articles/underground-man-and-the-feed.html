Near the end of the first part of Notes from Underground, its unnamed narrator makes a peculiar claim about the pages he is writing: “I shall never have readers.” He has already spent much of the book addressing “gentlemen,” anticipating their objections and revising his replies. He later admits that he may “purposely imagine an audience” because writing before one gives his performance greater dignity. The contradiction is present on the page. He denies having readers while allowing imagined readers to govern his voice.

Fyodor Dostoevsky published Notes from Underground in 1864. Its narrator is a retired civil servant living alone in St. Petersburg. He opens by calling himself sick and spiteful, then questions each description and supplies fresh versions of his motives. Constance Garnett’s public-domain translation preserves the abrupt reversals, qualifications and direct addresses that make the monologue feel argumentative even when nobody else is in the room.

A reader invented on the page

The narrator’s “gentlemen” have no biographies and take no independent action. Their role is to object. He tells them what they are thinking, mocks the answer he has assigned to them and then changes his ground. When he pauses to explain why he addresses readers he expects never to have, he offers several possibilities: the form helps him write, it lets him criticise himself and it may bring relief from a memory he cannot put aside.

This imagined exchange shapes more than the presentation of his ideas. It gives him an opponent against whom every admission can be staged. He can accuse his readers of misunderstanding him before they have responded. He can also expose his own vanity first, gaining some control over the accusation. The result is a monologue organised around anticipated reaction.

That feature supports a comparison with online writing. A post may be composed in answer to an expected critic long before any reply appears. The useful parallel concerns rhetoric and audience. Dostoevsky’s narrator demonstrates how an absent public can influence the words chosen in private.

Consciousness as a form of delay

The narrator describes excessive consciousness as an illness. His example is the “mouse,” a person who has suffered an insult and then spends years reconstructing it. In Garnett’s translation, the mouse remembers the injury in minute detail, adds further humiliations through imagination and considers acts of revenge that would injure the avenger most of all. Thought extends the original encounter far beyond its duration.

He treats this inability to act as evidence of refinement. Direct people can move because, in his account, they accept a cause and proceed towards it. He keeps looking behind each motive for an earlier one. His intelligence becomes a means of postponement and a source of status. He can regard himself as superior to the people who act while resenting them for their freedom from his analysis.

Read alongside online argument, the passage draws attention to anticipated motives, hidden incentives and possible humiliation. This essay treats that connection as an interpretive comparison. The text itself stays with one man’s effort to convert indecision into a mark of distinction.

Choice against self-interest

Part I argues with rational accounts of human behaviour. The narrator imagines systems in which science and calculation identify a person’s interests and guide conduct accordingly. He objects that a person may knowingly choose an injurious course in order to preserve the experience of choosing. His repeated attack on “twice two makes four” is directed at the claim that human desire can be contained by a complete formula.

The argument gives spite a specific function. Refusing sensible advice can prove, at least to the narrator, that he remains free from the person or system offering it. The cost of the choice may strengthen its appeal because the injury makes the refusal unmistakably his. He recognizes the damage and proceeds with it.

This passage can illuminate the decision to continue a public quarrel after its likely cost is clear. The analogy has limits. Digital platforms introduce audiences, incentives and automated distribution that belong to a different period and require evidence of their own. Dostoevsky supplies a close account of one motive: the satisfaction a person may take in resisting an outcome presented as reasonable and beneficial.

Confession and control

The second part of the book tests the narrator’s self-description against his treatment of other people. After a humiliating dinner, he meets Liza and delivers an elaborate speech about the life he believes awaits her. He imagines himself as her rescuer. When she later visits his shabby room and sees his dependence on his servant, he responds to his own embarrassment by insulting her. Before she leaves, he presses money into her hand.

His account of the episode is full of correction. He says he meant to describe the money as an accident, then records that he did it from spite. The admission is exact, and it arrives after the harm. Self-knowledge allows him to describe his cruelty in detail without giving Liza relief from it.

The scene also changes the meaning of his imagined audience. In Part I he controls both sides of a debate. Liza is physically present and responds in ways he has failed to script. Her compassion embarrasses him because it overturns the role he had assigned himself. His later narration restores his control over the order and explanation of events, while preserving evidence of how badly that control failed in the room.

The reach and limit of the comparison

Notes from Underground offers no theory of social media. It examines a narrator who performs for invented readers, interprets every action through possible humiliation and uses confession to manage judgement. Those features make the book useful for thinking about some forms of online speech. They cannot explain platform design, collective behaviour or the experience of every user.

The distinction keeps the comparison close to the text. Dostoevsky’s narrator writes because a memory has returned and he wants relief from it. Once he begins, the readers he claims never to have become part of the composition. The book leaves him recording, revising and addressing that audience from his room.
