On 19 July, U.S. Central Command said it had completed a ninth consecutive evening of strikes inside Iran. It listed military command centres, air-defence and coastal-surveillance sites, maritime capabilities, launch sites and communications networks among the targets. The command described the operation as an effort to reduce Iran’s ability to attack commercial ships and their crews. That is the United States’ account of its purpose and targeting; it is not an independent damage assessment.

What can be independently established is that commercial shipping has again been hit. The International Maritime Organization’s incident register listed 58 confirmed incidents in the Strait of Hormuz and the wider Middle East by 17 July, with 17 seafarer deaths. Its July entries include damaged ships off Oman on 6, 7, 11, 13, 14 and 16 July. A crew member was reported killed aboard Al Bahyah on 14 July, another aboard GFS Galaxy on 11 July, while eleven people were injured and Mombasa B abandoned. The IMO table records locations, harm and pollution; it generally does not assign responsibility. That distinction matters in a conflict where every side uses attribution to justify its next move.

A reopening that did not settle the war

The renewed violence followed a brief opening. UN Trade and Development recorded more than 100 days of disruption after the military escalation that began on 28 February. A mid-June agreement between Washington and Tehran included reopening the strait, and the U.S. Energy Information Administration reported that traffic subsequently increased. On 7 July, the EIA revised its oil outlook on the assumption that production and trade would continue returning towards pre-conflict levels.

That assumption was already under strain. CENTCOM said its 7–8 July strikes followed Iranian attacks on three commercial vessels. Iran, in a formal submission circulated through the IMO, has offered a sharply different legal and factual account: it says the strait remained open, that navigation had not been suspended and that its measures were necessary and proportionate acts of self-defence. Those are Iran’s claims. They sit uneasily beside the IMO’s record of damaged ships, a paused evacuation operation and the organization’s repeated warnings that crews could not be assured safe passage.

By 16 July, the IMO said around 20,000 seafarers, port workers and offshore personnel were affected by instability in the region. Around 6,000 seafarers had been stranded aboard vessels unable to leave the Gulf safely. Between 23 and 26 June, a voluntary IMO framework helped 136 vessels and an estimated 2,900 seafarers depart; the operation was later paused following another attack. These are not secondary effects at the edge of the conflict. Merchant crews have become the human buffer between military coercion, energy security and the legal promise of transit passage.

The rules are clear; enforcement is not

The Strait of Hormuz is not an improvised route. Its traffic-separation scheme was proposed by Iran and Oman and adopted by the IMO in 1968. On 13 July, the IMO Council reaffirmed that transit through straits used for international navigation must not be threatened, impeded or suspended. It called for non-discriminatory passage through the recognized lanes and said transit should remain free of tolls and charges. The Council also condemned attacks on civilian commercial ships and urged de-escalation.

The economic stakes explain the urgency. In the first half of 2025, before this year’s war, the strait carried an average 20.9 million barrels of oil a day, about one fifth of global petroleum-liquids consumption, according to the EIA. UNCTAD’s June assessment found that the 2026 disruption had already raised energy, fertilizer and freight costs. It warned that the damage would persist after reopening because transport prices and supply chains adjust more slowly than spot oil prices. Least-developed and small-island economies face a particularly hard combination: high dependence on imported fuel and food, tight public finances and limited room to absorb inflation.

The sequence since June demonstrates a wider vulnerability. A diplomatic announcement can reopen a chokepoint in market forecasts before it creates a durable security arrangement on the water. Insurers, masters and crews must decide against observable threats, not communiqués. When damaged hulls and fatalities reappear, traffic can thin again even without a formally declared closure.

Analysis: the strait has become leverage, not merely terrain

The immediate confrontation is military, but the strategic contest is over who can set conditions for ordinary movement. Iran’s position links navigation to what it calls self-defence against aggression. Washington has used strikes and coercive maritime measures to impose costs and protect passage on terms it defines. Gulf states and shipping organizations want predictable, non-discriminatory transit. These positions overlap only at the level of rhetoric: every actor says it favours safety, while their actions and legal claims produce incompatible operating rules.

Military suppression may reduce particular launchers or patrol craft, but it cannot by itself provide neutral incident attribution, crew evacuation, mine clearance, insurance confidence or a stable communications channel between littoral states. Conversely, a ceasefire that specifies reopening without a verified maritime mechanism leaves the most vulnerable part of the bargain exposed. The July relapse is evidence of that institutional gap, not proof that diplomacy is futile.

A durable arrangement would need mundane components: direct notification channels, agreed separation of military and commercial traffic, rapid independent investigation of ship strikes, reliable access for the IMO, and guarantees that seafarers can leave without becoming bargaining chips. None requires agreement on every cause of the wider war. All require the parties to treat navigation as a protected system rather than a reservoir of coercive pressure.

As of 20 July, the most defensible conclusion is restrained. The strait is functioning, but it is not secure; ships are moving, but crews are still being killed; and a ceasefire that briefly improved the energy outlook has not yet produced a maritime order capable of surviving the next disputed incident.
